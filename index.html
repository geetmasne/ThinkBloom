Remembering

The ability to recall facts, terms, basic concepts, and definitions from memory without necessarily understanding what they mean.

Cognitive Process: Retrieving relevant knowledge from long-term memory.

Key verbs: Define, List, Recall, Memorize, Repeat, State, Name, Identify, Match, Label, Recite

Examples:

  • Name the six levels of Bloom's Taxonomy.
  • List the first 10 elements in the periodic table.
  • Recite the multiplication table up to 12Ã—12.

Question Stems:

  • What is...?
  • Who was...?
  • When did...?
  • How many...?
  • Can you name...?
  • Where is...?

Learning Activities:

  • Flashcards
  • Memory games
  • Quizzes with basic recall questions
  • Labeling diagrams

Understanding

The ability to comprehend and interpret information, explain ideas or concepts, and translate knowledge into new contexts.

Cognitive Process: Constructing meaning from instructional messages, including oral, written, and graphic communication.

Key verbs: Classify, Describe, Explain, Identify, Summarize, Compare, Interpret, Discuss, Translate, Paraphrase, Infer, Exemplify

Examples:

  • Explain the difference between applying and analyzing in your own words.
  • Summarize the main points of the chapter on photosynthesis.
  • Compare and contrast democracy and autocracy.

Question Stems:

  • How would you explain...?
  • Can you describe what happens when...?
  • How would you summarize...?
  • What is the main idea of...?
  • Can you clarify...?
  • How would you differentiate between...?

Learning Activities:

  • Create concept maps
  • Restate concepts in your own words
  • Summarize news articles or chapters
  • Create analogies

Applying

The ability to use learned material in new and concrete situations, including applying rules, methods, concepts, principles, laws, and theories.

Cognitive Process: Carrying out or using a procedure in a given situation.

Key verbs: Apply, Demonstrate, Illustrate, Operate, Solve, Use, Calculate, Implement, Execute, Show, Modify, Prepare

Examples:

  • Use the principles of Bloom's Taxonomy to design a lesson plan.
  • Solve a set of quadratic equations using the formula.
  • Apply Newton's laws of motion to a real-world problem.

Question Stems:

  • How would you use...?
  • What examples can you find to...?
  • How would you solve ___ using what you've learned?
  • How would you organize ___ to show...?
  • How would you show your understanding of...?
  • What approach would you use to...?

Learning Activities:

  • Problem-solving exercises
  • Role-playing scenarios
  • Lab experiments
  • Using models to demonstrate knowledge

Analyzing

The ability to break down information into its components, seeing relationships and hierarchical structure, and distinguishing between facts and inferences.

Cognitive Process: Breaking material into constituent parts and determining how parts relate to one another and to an overall structure or purpose.

Key verbs: Analyze, Differentiate, Examine, Test, Compare, Contrast, Categorize, Investigate, Distinguish, Question, Deconstruct, Organize

Examples:

  • Analyze a test to determine which cognitive levels are being assessed.
  • Compare and contrast the themes in 'Romeo and Juliet' and 'West Side Story'.
  • Examine the relationship between global warming and extreme weather events.

Question Stems:

  • What are the parts or features of...?
  • How is ___ related to...?
  • Why do you think...?
  • What is the theme/underlying message of...?
  • What evidence can you find to support...?
  • What is the relationship between...?

Learning Activities:

  • SWOT analysis
  • Cause and effect diagrams
  • Literary analysis essays
  • Surveys and data analysis

Evaluating

The ability to make judgments based on criteria and standards through checking and critiquing, assessing validity and quality.

Cognitive Process: Making judgments based on criteria and standards.

Key verbs: Appraise, Critique, Evaluate, Judge, Support, Value, Defend, Assess, Conclude, Justify, Prioritize, Recommend

Examples:

  • Evaluate the effectiveness of using higher-order thinking questions in classroom instruction.
  • Judge the validity of arguments for and against climate change policies.
  • Critique a research paper's methodology and conclusions.

Question Stems:

  • Do you agree with the actions/outcomes...?
  • What is your opinion of...?
  • How would you prioritize...?
  • What criteria would you use to assess...?
  • What choice would you have made...?
  • How would you justify...?

Learning Activities:

  • Debates
  • Peer reviews
  • Writing persuasive arguments
  • Creating rubrics for assessment

Creating

The ability to put elements together to form a coherent whole; reorganizing elements into a new pattern or structure through generating, planning, or producing.

Cognitive Process: Putting elements together to form a novel, coherent whole or make an original product.

Key verbs: Create, Design, Develop, Formulate, Produce, Compose, Construct, Generate, Invent, Plan, Author, Devise

Examples:

  • Create a new assessment strategy incorporating all levels of Bloom's Taxonomy.
  • Design an experiment to test the effects of different fertilizers on plant growth.
  • Compose an original piece of music expressing a specific emotion.

Question Stems:

  • What could you invent to...?
  • How would you design a new...?
  • What might happen if you combined...?
  • How would you solve the problem in a different way?
  • How would you test your...?
  • Can you develop a proposal for...?

Learning Activities:

  • Project-based learning
  • Creating original stories or art
  • Designing prototypes or models
  • Formulating hypotheses and research studies
